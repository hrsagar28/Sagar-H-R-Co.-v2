TDS is the highest-volume compliance task in any business, and it is changing on two fronts. First, the **Finance Act, 2025** raised a raft of threshold limits with effect from **1 April 2025** (FY 2025-26). Second, the **Income-tax Act, 2025** completely restructured how TDS sections are numbered and reported from **1 April 2026** (Tax Year 2026-27). Deductors need to get both right — the thresholds now, the renumbering from next year.

:::summary

### Key Takeaways

- FA 2025 raised many TDS thresholds from 1 April 2025 — rent ₹50,000/month, professional fees ₹50,000, and a new Section 194T on partner payments.
- Section 206C(1H) TCS on sale of goods and the non-filer provisions (206AB / 206CCA) were withdrawn.
- From 1 April 2026, 40-plus TDS sections collapse into Sections 392 (salary), 393 (all other payments) and 394 (TCS), with payment codes replacing section numbers.
- Rates and due dates are largely unchanged and your PAN/TAN stay the same — the reform is structural.
  :::

## Part 1 — FA 2025 threshold hikes (apply from 1 April 2025)

These changes are already live for FY 2025-26. Revisit your vendor master, rent and lease agreements, and partner-remuneration structures against them. Deduct too little and you face interest and disallowance; deduct where the higher threshold now exempts the payment and you create needless friction.

| Section | Nature of payment                                                       | Revised threshold (from 1 April 2025) |
| ------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| 194A    | Interest (other than securities) — senior citizens                      | ₹1,00,000                             |
| 194A    | Interest from banks / co-ops / post office — others                     | ₹50,000                               |
| 194A    | Interest — all other cases                                              | ₹10,000                               |
| 194     | Dividend                                                                | ₹10,000                               |
| 194K    | Income from mutual fund units                                           | ₹10,000                               |
| 194D    | Insurance commission                                                    | ₹20,000                               |
| 194H    | Commission or brokerage                                                 | ₹20,000                               |
| 194-I   | Rent                                                                    | ₹50,000 **per month**                 |
| 194J    | Professional / technical fees                                           | ₹50,000                               |
| 194LA   | Compensation on land acquisition                                        | ₹5,00,000                             |
| 194T    | **New** — salary, remuneration, commission, bonus, interest to partners | 10% above ₹20,000 p.a.                |

Two structural changes also took effect from 1 April 2025:

- **TCS on sale of goods under Section 206C(1H) was withdrawn**, removing the awkward overlap with the buyer-side TDS on purchase of goods.
- **The higher-rate provisions for non-filers (Sections 206AB and 206CCA) were omitted**, simplifying vendor checks.
- The **LRS threshold for TCS on foreign remittances was raised to ₹10 lakh.**

**Section 194T deserves special attention.** Many partnership firms and LLPs had no history of deducting TDS on partner payments. From FY 2025-26, tax must be deducted at 10% on salary, remuneration, commission, bonus, or interest paid to a partner once the annual aggregate crosses ₹20,000. The firm is the deductor.

## Part 2 — The new Act renumbers everything (from 1 April 2026)

From Tax Year 2026-27, the entire "194-series" is retired. Over forty separate TDS sections collapse into just three parent provisions, now housed in **Chapter XIX** of the 2025 Act:

- **Section 392** — TDS on salary (old Section 192).
- **Section 393** — TDS on **every** other payment to residents and non-residents, presented as a set of tables covering contractors, professionals, rent, interest, commission, e-commerce, virtual digital assets and more.
- **Section 394** — Tax Collected at Source (old Section 206C).

**Rates and thresholds are largely preserved** — the reform is structural, not a rate change. The 1% on contractor payments is still 1%; professional fees are still 10%. What changes is _how you cite and report_ the deduction.

### The practical mechanics that will catch people out

- **Payment codes replace section numbers.** On challans and quarterly returns, you now select a numeric payment code that corresponds to a specific table entry in Section 393, rather than quoting "194C" or "194J." **Quoting an old section number for a payment made on or after 1 April 2026 will trigger validation errors on the portal** and force a correction statement. _(The exact code numbers circulating online are inconsistent — take them only from your updated, CBDT-aligned TDS software or the official notification.)_
- **Forms are renumbered.** Among the changes: Form 16 → Form 130, Form 16A → Form 131, Form 24Q → Form 138, Form 26Q → Form 140, Form 27Q → Form 144, Forms 15G/15H merged into Form 121, Form 13 (lower-deduction application) → Form 128, and Form 26AS → Form 168.
- **Which Act applies is decided by "earlier of credit or payment."** If that earlier event falls on or before 31 March 2026, the 1961 Act governs; from 1 April 2026, the 2025 Act applies. Once tax has been deducted under the old Act on credit, no fresh deduction is needed on later payment of the same sum.
- **Due dates are unchanged.** The deposit timelines carry over — including 30 April for tax deducted in March (non-government deductors).
- **Existing lower-deduction certificates stay valid.** A Section 197 certificate issued for projected receivables of Tax Year 2026-27 remains effective for payments on or after 1 April 2026.
- **Manpower supply is now clearly "work."** The new Act resolves the old ambiguity: deploying contract workers / labour-supply services falls within the contractor-TDS provision. If you were not deducting on such contracts, correct that.
- **CBDT guidance now binds.** Certain CBDT guidelines on TDS matters carry mandatory effect for deductors — the "circulars are only advisory" argument no longer holds in those areas.

## Your action list

1. **Now (FY 2025-26):** confirm your systems reflect the FA 2025 thresholds, especially the new Section 194T on partner payments.
2. **Before the first Tax Year 2026-27 return:** update payroll and ERP to the new section references, load the correct Section 393 payment codes, switch to the new form numbers, and review manpower-supply contracts.
3. **Throughout:** watch for CBDT transition circulars, which continue to be issued.

Your PAN and **TAN remain unchanged** — no fresh registration is required.
