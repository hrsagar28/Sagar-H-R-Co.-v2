Property has always been the largest capital-gains decision most families face, and the **Finance (No. 2) Act, 2024** rewrote the rules mid-year. For every sale of land or building registered **on or after 23 July 2024**, long-term capital gains are now taxed at a flat **12.5% without indexation**, replacing the old **20% with indexation** regime. A narrow but valuable grandfathering option survives for some sellers. Under the Income-tax Act, 2025 the substance carries forward — with the section numbers renumbered. Here is how it all fits together.

:::summary

### Key Takeaways

- For property sold on or after 23 July 2024, LTCG is taxed at a flat 12.5% without indexation (was 20% with indexation).
- Resident individuals and HUFs selling property acquired before 23 July 2024 can choose the lower of 12.5% without indexation or 20% with indexation.
- NRIs, OCIs, companies, LLPs and firms don’t get the grandfathering option — only 12.5% without indexation applies.
- As a rough guide, 12.5% wins when the sale price exceeds ~4× the purchase price — so compute both and pay the lower.
  :::

## The core change

- **New default:** LTCG on land/building transferred on or after 23 July 2024 is taxed at **12.5%, with no indexation.** The gain is simply sale price minus cost of acquisition minus cost of improvement minus transfer expenses — no Cost Inflation Index, no indexed cost.
- **Old regime:** the previous 20%-with-indexation rate, which adjusted the purchase price for inflation, no longer applies to fresh transfers.

Removing indexation is a double-edged change. The headline rate is lower, but without an inflation adjustment the _taxable_ gain can be larger — so whether you pay more or less depends entirely on how long you held and how much the property appreciated.

## The grandfathering option — who gets it

To avoid penalising long-term owners, an amendment introduced a transitional choice (the **second proviso to Section 112** of the 1961 Act):

- **Eligible:** **resident individuals and HUFs**, on **land or building acquired before 23 July 2024**.
- **The benefit:** for such property, tax is the **lower of** (a) 12.5% without indexation, or (b) 20% with indexation.
- **Not eligible:** **NRIs and OCIs, companies, LLPs, and firms**, and it does **not** extend to assets other than land and building. For those, only the 12.5%-without-indexation route is available.

Two practical wrinkles: a **loss** computed under the indexation route generally **cannot be carried forward or set off** if the 12.5% route is what you ultimately use — the grandfathering caps the _tax_, it does not restore a notional loss. And for very old property, the cost of acquisition can be taken as the higher of actual cost or **fair market value as on 1 April 2001**, with the FMV of land capped at its stamp-duty value as on that date.

## When is 12.5% actually better?

As a rough rule of thumb, **12.5% without indexation tends to win when the sale price is more than roughly four times the purchase price**; below that, 20% with indexation often produces a lower bill. The exact break-even shifts with the holding period and the applicable CII, so for any eligible property the discipline is the same: **compute both, pay the lower.** A short illustration makes the point.

_Resident individual, land acquired for ₹35 lakh, sold for ₹140 lakh (with some improvement and transfer costs):_

- **Route A — 12.5% without indexation:** tax on the plain gain works out to roughly **₹12 lakh** (plus cess).
- **Route B — 20% with indexation:** after indexing the cost, the taxable gain shrinks, and tax works out lower — around **₹10 lakh** (plus cess).

Here a resident would elect Route B. An NRI on identical facts has no choice and must use Route A. The figures are illustrative — the point is that the answer flips case by case.

## Holding periods and listed securities

- **Land and building** are long-term if held for **more than 24 months.** The earlier 36-month category has been removed.
- For context, **listed equity shares and equity mutual funds** are long-term after 12 months; their **LTCG is taxed at 12.5%** with an annual exemption of **₹1.25 lakh**, and **STCG at 20%.**

## Exemptions still work

The reinvestment exemptions are intact regardless of which route you use — **Section 54** (reinvesting in a residential house), **Section 54F** (net-consideration reinvestment), and **Section 54EC** (specified bonds, capped at ₹50 lakh). One consequence of losing indexation: because the computed gain can be higher, the amount you need to reinvest to fully shelter it may also be higher, so plan the reinvestment quantum carefully.

## How this reads under the Income-tax Act, 2025

From Tax Year 2026-27, the capital-gains provisions are **renumbered but substantively continued.** The charge of capital gains, which sat under **Section 45** of the 1961 Act, is carried into **Section 67** of the 2025 Act, with the computation and exemption logic preserved. The 12.5%/indexation architecture and the grandfathering choice continue to apply as above; only the citations move. As always for the new Act, verify the exact new section reference against the official gazette before quoting it in a document.

## A word for NRI sellers

NRIs do not get the grandfathering option and are taxed under the new regime only. TDS on an NRI's property sale is deducted on the **gross consideration** at the applicable rate unless a **lower-deduction certificate** (under Section 197 of the old Act / its new-Act equivalent) is obtained **before** the sale. Applying for that certificate ahead of the transaction can prevent a large amount of tax being locked up until the return is filed.

## The takeaway

For resident individuals and HUFs selling **pre-23-July-2024** property, always run both computations — the grandfathering option can save real money, especially on moderately appreciated holdings. For everyone else, and for any post-cut-off purchase, it is a flat 12.5% without indexation. And whichever route applies, the reinvestment exemptions remain your best lever to reduce the bill.
