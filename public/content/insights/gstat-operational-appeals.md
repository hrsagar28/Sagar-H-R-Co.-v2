For eight years after GST launched, one crucial piece of the dispute-resolution machinery was missing. A taxpayer who lost before the First Appellate Authority had nowhere to go except the High Court — expensive, slow, and not designed for routine tax disputes. That gap has now closed. The **Goods and Services Tax Appellate Tribunal (GSTAT)** was formally launched on **24 September 2025** and began hearing cases from **16 February 2026.** This changes GST litigation strategy for every business.

:::summary

### Key Takeaways

- GSTAT began hearing cases from 16 February 2026, completing the appeal chain between the First Appellate Authority and the High Court.
- Backlog cut-off: for orders communicated before 1 May 2026, appeals must be filed at GSTAT by 31 July 2026 — treat it as a hard deadline.
- Pre-deposit is an additional 10% of the disputed tax only, paid through the Electronic Cash Ledger (ITC cannot be used).
- Filing is fully online via Form GST APL-05, with all documents uploaded up front and precise, provision-anchored grounds.
  :::

## Where GSTAT sits in the appeal chain

The route is now complete and tiered:

**Adjudicating officer → First Appellate Authority (Section 107) → GSTAT (Section 112) → High Court → Supreme Court.**

GSTAT operates as a two-tier body: a **Principal Bench in New Delhi** and **State Benches** across the country. For Mysuru-based businesses, Karnataka matters are heard at the **State Bench in Bengaluru.** The Principal Bench also doubles as the **National Appellate Authority for Advance Rulings**, deciding conflicts between the advance rulings of different states.

## The deadline that cannot be missed

There are two timelines running in parallel, and confusing them is costly:

- **Normal rule:** an appeal to GSTAT must be filed within **three months** of the First Appellate Authority's (or Revisional Authority's) order being communicated. A delay of up to a further three months may be condoned — but that is discretionary, not automatic.
- **Backlog window (extended).** For the enormous pile of orders that accumulated while the Tribunal did not exist, a one-time window applies. The Government had originally set 30 June 2026, but by **Notification S.O. 3502(E) dated 30 June 2026** (superseding the earlier 17 September 2025 notification), that date was **extended by one month to 31 July 2026** in the face of a last-minute surge of filings. So: **for any appeal against an order communicated before 1 May 2026, the outer deadline to file at GSTAT is 31 July 2026.** For orders communicated on or after 1 May 2026, only the normal three-month rule applies. (For departmental applications under Section 112(3), the corresponding cut-off is orders passed before 1 February 2026.)

**Action point:** every business sitting on an unfavourable First Appellate Authority order — however old — should check its communication date now and confirm whether it falls within the 31 July 2026 window. The Government has advised filing well ahead rather than at the deadline, and High Courts have started declining GST writ petitions in matters that should go to GSTAT, so this is the live remedy. Treat 31 July 2026 as a hard cut-off — there is no indication of any further extension.

## The pre-deposit — get the amount right

The mandatory pre-deposit is the single most common source of error.

- **At the GSTAT stage,** you deposit an **additional 10% of the disputed tax** (over and above the 10% already paid at the first-appeal stage), subject to a **cap of ₹20 crore each for CGST and SGST** (₹40 crore for IGST). This was reduced from 20% by the Finance (No. 2) Act, 2024. Many taxpayers wrongly compute it on the _original_ demand, or as a fresh full amount, rather than on the _remaining disputed_ amount after netting what was already paid.
- **The percentage is on disputed tax only.** Admitted amounts must be paid in full, but disputed _interest_ and _penalty_ are not separately loaded into the 10% base — a point confirmed in case law.
- **Penalty-only appeals** (orders imposing only penalty, with no tax demand) require **10% of the disputed penalty** — a requirement introduced with effect from **1 October 2025**, and applying prospectively.
- **Small disputes may not be admitted.** GSTAT can decline an appeal where the total amount involved is **₹50,000 or less.**
- **Pay through the Electronic Cash Ledger only.** ITC (the Electronic Credit Ledger) **cannot** be used for the pre-deposit — a frequent and expensive misconception. Payment is made via Bharatkosh.
- Any amount already deposited under a High Court interim order (while the Tribunal was non-functional) is adjusted against the pre-deposit requirement.

## Filing: it's a fully digital process

- Appeals are filed **online on the dedicated GSTAT e-filing portal**, using **Form GST APL-05.** Manual filing is not permitted without special permission.
- **All mandatory documents must be uploaded at the time of filing** — you cannot add them later except with the Tribunal's permission. Each PDF should be within the prescribed size limit.
- The filing is authenticated using a **DSC or Aadhaar e-sign**, and an acknowledgement with a filing number is issued. **Treat the final acknowledgement — not a provisional one — as proof of valid filing.** A defective filing may not be admitted even if submitted before the deadline.
- **Appeal fees** are calculated on the disputed amount (broadly ₹1,000 per ₹1 lakh of tax in dispute, within a floor and ceiling).

## A helpful transitional relaxation

Recognising that the portal and the ecosystem are still stabilising, GSTAT has extended a **relaxed-scrutiny regime for filings until 31 December 2026** — with registries directed to raise defects of substance rather than form (for example, accepting properly endorsed scanned certified copies, and not flagging documents generated digitally through the GST system). This is welcome breathing room, but it is **not** a reason to file loose grounds.

## Drafting that survives scrutiny

The Tribunal's registry can return defective filings, and vague grounds get nowhere. Grounds such as "the order is incorrect" or "the demand is excessive" are insufficient. **Each ground must identify the exact provision of law, the specific error committed, and the relief sought**, supported by the facts and precedents. GSTAT is a judicial forum; treat the drafting accordingly. Orders are to be pronounced within a defined period after the final hearing and are published on the portal.

## The takeaway

GSTAT gives businesses a specialised, dedicated forum between the First Appellate Authority and the High Court — a genuine improvement in access to justice. But the opportunity comes with hard mechanics: the **31 July 2026 backlog cut-off**, the **correctly computed pre-deposit paid only through the cash ledger**, complete documents at filing, and precise, provision-anchored grounds. If you have pending or contemplated GST appeals, this is the moment to review them.
