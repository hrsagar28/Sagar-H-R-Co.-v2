For more than six decades, India's direct tax system ran on the Income-tax Act, 1961. That era has ended. The **Income-tax Act, 2025** (Act No. 30 of 2025) received Presidential assent on **21 August 2025** and came into force on **1 April 2026**, repealing the 1961 Act and replacing it as the charging statute for income tax in India.

For clients and practitioners alike, the natural question is: _how much of my tax life actually changes?_ The short answer is that the **architecture** has changed a great deal, while the **substance** — what you pay and on what — has largely been preserved. This note separates the two.

:::summary

### Key Takeaways

- The Income-tax Act, 2025 (Act No. 30 of 2025) replaced the 1961 Act from 1 April 2026 — a re-codification into 536 sections, not a new tax.
- Tax rates and slabs are unchanged; they remain a Finance Act matter, revised every year.
- Almost every section is renumbered (80C → 123, 139 → 263, 115BAC → 202), and 40-plus TDS sections collapse into Sections 392–394.
- FY 2025-26 (AY 2026-27) income is still governed by the 1961 Act; the new Act applies only from Tax Year 2026-27.
  :::

## The headline: a re-codification, not a new tax

The new Act consolidates the law into **536 sections across 23 chapters and 16 schedules**, down from the 819 sections the 1961 Act had accumulated through decades of amendment. The stated purpose is simplification: cleaner drafting, fewer provisos and explanations, a more logical sequence, and language designed to reduce interpretation disputes and litigation.

Critically, the Government has been explicit that the 2025 Act **does not impose any new tax and does not, by itself, alter tax rates**. Rates continue to be fixed each year by the annual Finance Act — and indeed the 2025 Act already stands amended by the Finance Act, 2026.

## What changes from 1 April 2026

- **Terminology.** The twin concepts of "Previous Year" and "Assessment Year" are replaced by a single **"Tax Year."** (We cover this in a separate note.)
- **Section numbers.** Almost every familiar section has a new number. Section 80C is now Section 123; the return of income (Section 139) is now Section 263; the new tax regime (Section 115BAC) is now Section 202; capital gains (Section 45) sit under Section 67. The concepts survive; the citations move.
- **TDS/TCS structure.** More than forty scattered TDS sections have been folded into just three parent sections — **Section 392 (salary), Section 393 (all other deductions), and Section 394 (TCS)** — using a tabular, code-driven format.
- **Forms.** Many long-standing forms have been renumbered under the new Income-tax Rules. Form 16 becomes Form 130, Form 16A becomes Form 131, and Form 26AS is replaced by the more detailed Form 168, among others.
- **Digital-first administration.** The Act leans into system-driven compliance, faceless processes, and consolidated annual information statements.
- **Binding effect of CBDT guidance.** Under the new framework, certain CBDT guidelines carry mandatory weight for both the department and deductors — the old argument that "circulars are merely advisory" no longer holds in those areas.

## What does _not_ change

- **Tax rates and slabs.** These remain a Finance Act matter, revised annually.
- **The core scheme.** Heads of income, the residence rules, the basis of charge, and the fundamental computation logic are carried forward.
- **PAN and TAN.** Your existing PAN and TAN continue unchanged. No fresh registration is required.
- **Deductions.** Chapter VI-A deductions (80C, 80D and the rest) are renumbered, **not repealed**, and continue to be available under the old regime.
- **Exemptions.** The old Section 10 catalogue of exempt incomes has been relocated to **Schedule II**, but the exemptions themselves largely continue.
- **TDS due dates.** The deposit timelines remain the same; the corresponding rule under the new Rules simply mirrors the old one.
- **Past years and pending matters.** The repeal of the 1961 Act does not disturb anything relating to periods before 1 April 2026. Completed assessments stay valid, and pending proceedings, appeals, and rectifications continue under the 1961 Act through the transitional provisions.

## The transition line every taxpayer should remember

The single most important boundary is this: **income earned during FY 2025-26 (AY 2026-27) is still governed by the Income-tax Act, 1961.** Even though those returns are filed _after_ 1 April 2026, they use the old Act and old section numbers. The 2025 Act applies **prospectively** to income earned from 1 April 2026 onwards — i.e. Tax Year 2026-27 and beyond.

The Act also contains detailed transition provisions so that tax credits, brought-forward losses, and unabsorbed depreciation carry across the changeover without loss.

## What this means for you

Practically, the change is more about **references and systems** than about liability. Payroll software, ERP masters, engagement templates, contracts that cite specific sections, and TDS configurations all need updating for Tax Year 2026-27 — but the tax outcome for a given transaction should be substantially the same as before. The work is in getting the plumbing right; the water is the same.
