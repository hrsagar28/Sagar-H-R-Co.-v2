One of the most visible changes under the Income-tax Act, 2025 is also one of the simplest to explain — and one of the most welcome. From 1 April 2026, the two overlapping timelines that every taxpayer had to juggle, the **"Previous Year"** and the **"Assessment Year,"** are gone. In their place is a single, unified concept: the **Tax Year.**

:::summary

### Key Takeaways

- From 1 April 2026 the “Previous Year” and “Assessment Year” are replaced by a single “Tax Year.”
- Tax Year = the old Previous Year; income is still assessed after the year ends — only the extra “Assessment Year” label is gone.
- No year is skipped or double-counted: FY 2025-26 stays under the 1961 Act; income from 1 April 2026 falls under Tax Year 2026-27.
- A newly set-up business’s first Tax Year runs from its start date to 31 March.
  :::

## The old two-timeline system

Under the 1961 Act, income was earned in the _Previous Year_ (the financial year in which you earned it) but taxed in the following _Assessment Year_. So income earned in FY 2024-25 was assessed in AY 2025-26. This one-year offset was a constant source of confusion — clients routinely mixed up which year a form, notice, or deadline referred to, and even seasoned filers had to pause to translate.

## What a Tax Year is

The new Act defines a **Tax Year as a period of twelve months within a financial year.** It replaces the "Previous Year" — and the term "Assessment Year" is discontinued altogether. The charge of income-tax is now simply on the **total income of the Tax Year** of a person, at the rates fixed by the relevant Finance Act.

In plain terms: **Tax Year = the old Previous Year.** Income of a Tax Year continues to be assessed _after_ that year ends, exactly as before. What has been removed is the separate "Assessment Year" label sitting on top of it.

## The special case of a new business

If a business is newly set up, or a new source of income comes into existence part-way through a financial year, the Tax Year for it **begins on the date of setting up (or the date the source arises) and ends on 31 March** of that financial year. For example, a business started on 1 December 2026 has a Tax Year running from 1 December 2026 to 31 March 2027.

## There is no missing year and no overlap

A common worry during any such transition is whether a year gets skipped or double-counted. It does not. The changeover works cleanly:

- **Income earned during FY 2025-26** is governed by the **Income-tax Act, 1961** and assessed in **AY 2026-27** (old terminology).
- **Income earned from 1 April 2026 onwards** is governed by the **Income-tax Act, 2025** and falls under **Tax Year 2026-27** and beyond.

Every rupee of income has exactly one home.

## Reading old references under the new Act

To keep historical citations coherent, the Act includes a transitional rule: **a reference to a "Tax Year" is read as a reference to the corresponding "Previous Year" under the old Act.** So if a provision speaks of "Tax Year 2024-25," that maps to "Previous Year 2024-25," which in old terms corresponds to Assessment Year 2025-26. This is purely a translation device — it does not change the tax treatment of those earlier years.

## Why the change is more than cosmetic

A single timeline delivers real, practical benefits:

- **Cleaner deadline counting.** Correction and compliance windows — such as the 48-month updated-return (ITR-U) window — are now measured from the end of the Tax Year, with no assessment-year offset to trip over.
- **Fewer errors.** There is no longer any ambiguity about "which year applied" on a form, challan, or notice.
- **Global alignment.** Most tax systems worldwide use a single tax-year concept. This is a meaningful simplification for NRIs, returning residents, and businesses with cross-border reporting.

## The bottom line

If the phrase "Assessment Year" is burned into your memory from years of filing, you can retire it for income earned from 1 April 2026. Think in one timeline now: the Tax Year in which income arises, assessed after that year closes. It is the same rhythm as before — just with one label instead of two.
