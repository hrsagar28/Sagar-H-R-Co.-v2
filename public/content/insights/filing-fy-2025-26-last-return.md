The return you file this year is a milestone: for most taxpayers, **FY 2025-26 (Assessment Year 2026-27) is the final year assessed under the Income-tax Act, 1961.** From FY 2026-27 (Tax Year 2026-27) onwards, the Income-tax Act, 2025 takes over. This note covers what you need to get this last "old-Act" return right — the deadline, the regime decision, and the documents to keep ready.

:::summary

### Key Takeaways

- FY 2025-26 (AY 2026-27) is the last return assessed under the 1961 Act, using the old section numbers — no conversion needed.
- New split deadlines: 31 July 2026 (salaried), 31 August 2026 (non-audit business/profession), 31 October 2026 (audit cases).
- File before the due date if you want the old regime — a belated return is taxed under the new regime by default.
- The new regime is the default; income up to ₹12 lakh is effectively tax-free (≈₹12.75 lakh for salaried after the ₹75,000 standard deduction).
  :::

## First, a point that trips people up

Even though you are filing in 2026 — _after_ the new Act came into force on 1 April 2026 — **this return is governed entirely by the 1961 Act and uses the old section numbers.** Your Form 16 for FY 2025-26 will carry old references, and the portal handles FY 2025-26 under the old framework. The new section numbers and forms only kick in from Tax Year 2026-27 returns. So there is no need to "convert" anything for this filing.

## The due dates — note the new split

There is an important change this year. The Finance Act, 2026 introduced a **separate, later due date for non-audit business and professional filers**, so the deadlines for FY 2025-26 (AY 2026-27) are no longer a single "non-audit" date:

- **31 July 2026** — salaried individuals and other non-business taxpayers (ITR-1 and ITR-2).
- **31 August 2026** — business and professional taxpayers **not** requiring audit (ITR-3 and ITR-4). This extra month is a new statutory provision, **not** a discretionary extension.
- **31 October 2026** — taxpayers whose accounts require audit (and their partners). The tax-audit report itself is due one month earlier, by **30 September 2026.**
- **30 November 2026** — taxpayers required to furnish a transfer-pricing report.

If you miss your due date, a **belated return** can generally be filed up to **31 December 2026**, with a late fee (Section 234F) and loss of certain benefits, such as the carry-forward of most losses. Separately, the Budget 2026 changes extended the **revised-return** window for AY 2026-27 to **31 March 2027** (from the earlier 31 December cut-off), giving more room to correct a filed return.

A word of caution regardless of your category: **file before the due date if you want the old regime.** A belated return is taxed under the new regime by default — you cannot opt into the old regime once you are late.

## The regime decision

For AY 2026-27, the **new tax regime is the default.** Under the new regime for FY 2025-26, the rebate under Section 87A makes income up to **₹12 lakh effectively tax-free** (a rebate of up to ₹60,000), and salaried taxpayers also get a **standard deduction of ₹75,000** — pushing the effective no-tax threshold for salary earners to around ₹12.75 lakh.

The **old regime remains available** for those it suits better — typically taxpayers with substantial home-loan interest, HRA, and 80C/80D investments. Key points:

- If you have **business or professional income** and want the old regime, you must file **Form 10-IEA** before the return due date.
- Salaried taxpayers without business income can choose between regimes **each year**. Taxpayers with business income face a much tighter rule — the option to move to the old regime is effectively a once-in-a-lifetime choice, and switching back to the new regime is limited, so decide deliberately rather than year-to-year.

The right choice is arithmetic, not ideology. Run both computations before deciding — the gap can be significant either way.

## Documents to keep ready

Gather these before you start so the filing is clean and defensible:

- **Form 16** (salary) and **Form 16A** (other TDS certificates).
- **Annual Information Statement (AIS)** and **Form 26AS** — reconcile every entry against your own records.
- **Interest certificates** — bank/FD interest, savings interest, and any interest paid on housing or education loans.
- **Capital gains statements** — broker/AMC statements for shares and mutual funds; sale deeds and cost records for property.
- **Deduction proofs** — 80C investments (LIC, PF/PPF, ELSS, tuition fees, housing-loan principal), 80D health-insurance premia, 80CCD(1B) NPS, donations, and so on.
- **Rent receipts / HRA workings** and, if applicable, the landlord's PAN where required.
- **Foreign assets and income details**, if any, for the required disclosures.

## Made a mistake? The updated-return safety net

If you later discover an omission in this final old-Act return, the **updated return (ITR-U)** route is available. For AY 2026-27, the ITR-U window opens after the assessment year ends and runs for 48 months — but the additional tax rises the longer you wait. Filing early, and filing correctly the first time, is always cheaper. (See our separate note on the 48-month ITR-U window.)

## The takeaway

Treat this return with the care of a final chapter. Reconcile against AIS and 26AS, pick the regime on the numbers, file by your due date (31 July for salaried filers, 31 August for non-audit business and professional filers), and keep your documentation tidy. Doing it well now leaves a clean base as you step into the Income-tax Act, 2025 next year.
