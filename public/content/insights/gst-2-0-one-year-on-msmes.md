The biggest overhaul of India's indirect tax system since 2017 arrived on **22 September 2025**, when the rate structure approved at the **56th GST Council meeting** (3 September 2025) came into force. Nearly a year of lived experience later, the headline reform — a move toward **two main slabs** — has settled into daily practice. The rate cuts grabbed the attention; the operational lessons are what separate a smooth MSME from one still cleaning up mismatches.

:::summary

### Key Takeaways

- From 22 September 2025, GST moved to two principal slabs — 5% and 18% — with 12% and 28% abolished and a 40% slab for luxury/sin goods.
- A rate cut alone does NOT require ITC reversal; reverse credit only where a supply became exempt.
- Watch value thresholds (e.g. textiles at ₹2,500) and update HSN/SAC rate masters in your billing/ERP.
- Track inverted-duty credit build-up and pursue refunds rather than letting it accumulate.
  :::

## What actually changed

The old five-rate structure (0%, 5%, 12%, 18%, 28% plus cess) was rationalised into a leaner design:

- **Two principal slabs — 5% and 18%** — now carry the vast majority of goods and services.
- **The 12% and 28% slabs were abolished.** Most 12% items moved down to 5%; most 28% items moved to 18%.
- **A 40% slab** was introduced for select luxury and "sin" goods — such as tobacco products, pan masala, aerated/energy drinks, high-end vehicles and large SUVs, and certain premium categories.
- **0% (nil)** continues and, in fact, expanded — individual **health and life insurance premiums** became exempt, along with a set of lifesaving medicines and educational stationery.
- **Compensation cess** was phased out for most categories, with tobacco and related goods on a separate transition track.

For a Mysuru MSME, the everyday effect has been mixed and category-specific: many mass-use goods got cheaper, several services (salons, gyms) dropped to 5%, while some items — notably higher-value textiles above the ₹2,500 threshold — moved up to 18%.

## Lesson 1 — Pricing and the transition-stock problem

The rate change had **no grace period** for most goods, which made the cut-over date decisive. The recurring pain points MSMEs learned the hard way:

- **Stock bought at the old rate and sold at the new rate** creates a tax-incidence mismatch that has to be handled carefully in pricing and accounting.
- **Price revisions must be documented.** Where output rates fell, the benefit was expected to be passed on; keeping a clear paper trail of cost-and-price workings is the practical defence against any anti-profiteering question.
- **Contracts and quotations** that were priced "inclusive of GST" needed revisiting, because the tax component moved.

## Lesson 2 — Input Tax Credit: reverse only when you must

This is where the most avoidable errors happened. The governing principle is simple but widely misunderstood:

- **A mere reduction in the output rate does NOT require any ITC reversal.** If your product moved from 12% to 5%, you do **not** surrender credit. Many businesses reversed credit unnecessarily and gave up money they were entitled to.
- **ITC reversal is required only where a supply became exempt** from 22 September 2025 (for example, where an output moved to nil-rated). Insurers, for instance, had to reassess credit because individual health/life insurance became exempt.
- **Inverted duty structures** appeared or sharpened in some sectors — where inputs sit at 18% but the finished output is now at 5% (parts of textiles, for example). Where you are in that position, track the accumulated credit and evaluate the refund route rather than letting it pile up.

## Lesson 3 — Classification is still where disputes are born

Fewer slabs reduce ambiguity, but they do not eliminate it. The classification discipline that matters:

- **Re-map every product and service to the correct HSN/SAC and current rate.** Slab summaries are a starting point, not an authority — check the specific notification for your goods.
- **Mind the value-based thresholds.** The textile line at ₹2,500 is the obvious example: the same category can attract different rates on either side of a price point.
- **Update your rate masters in billing/ERP software.** One wrong invoice creates a mismatch for your buyer and correction pressure for you. Automated, notification-aligned rate masters are the cheapest insurance against this.
- **Watch the auto-population.** Correct classification flows through to GSTR-1 and GSTR-3B; a wrong rate at source propagates into your returns.

## The MSME checklist a year in

1. **Confirm your rate masters** reflect every post-22-September change for the goods and services you actually deal in.
2. **Audit your ITC position** — recover any credit reversed unnecessarily, and reverse only where a supply genuinely became exempt.
3. **Flag inverted-duty exposure** and pursue refunds where accumulated credit is building.
4. **Keep pricing documentation** for the transition, ready for any query.
5. **Reconcile relentlessly** — invoice-level matching between your books, GSTR-1, GSTR-3B, and your suppliers' filings.

## The bottom line

GST 2.0 delivered a simpler rate map and, for many, lower rates. But the compliance edge belongs to businesses that got the _mechanics_ right: correct classification, disciplined ITC (reversing only when a supply became exempt), documented pricing, and clean reconciliation. If any of these are still loose ends for your business, the anniversary of the reform is a good moment to close them.
